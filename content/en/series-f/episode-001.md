## A Teacher's Lesson Resonating Across Half a Century

Half a century has passed since he moved to Hawaii and left his alma mater. 
This contribution written by Mr. Taro Higa in 1972 profoundly illustrates how a hometown school served as a spiritual sanctuary for "immigrants" living in a distant land.

## A Lesson Entrusted to the Banyan Tree

What Mr. Higa still remembers vividly is a lesson taught by Mr. Shosei Kina when he was in the third grade.

> "Even with the branches of a banyan tree, the young ones can be bent as you wish, but if you try to bend the old ones, they will simply snap. You too must study hard while you are young, for if you regret it after becoming an adult, it will be too late."

The banyan tree seen through the classroom window. This lesson, etched alongside that scenery, became a source of vitality that constantly supported Mr. Higa through both the hardships and joys of life in Hawaii.

## Hometown as a "Mother's Embrace"

In 1972, visiting his alma mater for the first time in 28 years, Mr. Higa wrote that he felt his hometown was "as warm as a mother's embrace."

Many Okinawans moved to Hawaii and South America after the war. For them, stepping onto Okinawan soil and seeing the children at their old school was a sacred ritual to reaffirm their own identity.

At "Ryukyu no Tane," we archive these memories of Okinawa scattered across the world and connect them to the next generation.
